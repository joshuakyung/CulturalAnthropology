Why Does Culture Matter for Ministry?

SLIDE 4

The core conviction of this course: the question is not whether culture matters — the question is whether we are paying attention to it.

01
The gospel travels through culture
There is no culture-free gospel delivery. It is always received by people shaped by language, food, family structure, honor systems, and religious tradition.
▼ expand
02
What feels "normal" may be cultural, not scriptural
The way you pray, preach, or organize a church carries cultural assumptions. This is worth examining — cultural practices are often invisible to those inside them.
▼ expand
03
Your audience reads through their lens, not yours
What seems clear to you may feel foreign to your listeners. Communication is not just what you say — it is what they hear, filtered through their cultural framework.
▼ expand
04
Cultural blindness has caused real harm
Imposing one culture's Christianity on another people is not the gospel. Mission history shows that cultural ignorance can produce colonial Christianity — not kingdom transformation.
▼ expand

What Is Cultural Anthropology?

SLIDE 7

Cultural anthropology is the academic study of human cultures — their beliefs, values, practices, symbols, and social structures. For missionaries and pastors, it means developing one key habit: moving from what to why.

SURFACE
❓ What is happening?
Describing behaviors, customs, and practices without interpreting their meaning. The tourist's lens.
DEPTH
🎯 Why do people do this?
Understanding meaning, motivation, and worldview behind behaviors. The anthropologist's lens — and the missionary's essential skill.
🎵

Field Case: K-Pop & Muslim Identity in Indonesia

SLIDES 8–9
Case Study — Cultural Anthropology in Action

Two Groups, Same Dance — Very Different Meanings

🇻🇳 Vietnamese Girls

K-pop cover dance in a public space. Culturally familiar activity — no significant social tension. Dancing is expressive, recreational, and community-building.

🇮🇩 Indonesian Muslim Girls

Girls in hijab performing K-pop in public. This is not a neutral act. It sparks social and religious conflict — negotiating identity, religion, globalization, and peer belonging simultaneously.

→ The question is not what is happening. The question is why. What are these young women negotiating? What does this act mean inside their world? This is cultural anthropology in practice.

"True knowledge comes with deep understanding of a topic and its inner workings."
— Albert Einstein  ·  Applied to Cross-Cultural Ministry

Defining Culture: Two Scholarly Anchors

SLIDE 16
Paul Hiebert — Textbook Author
"The more or less integrated systems of beliefs, feelings, and values — and their associated symbols, patterns of behavior, and products — shared by a group of people."

Five Properties of Culture

SLIDE 18

Click each card to expand the explanation.

🧠
01
Learned
Culture is not biological. It is absorbed through family, language, and community. It feels like instinct — but it is always learned, never innate.
👥
02
Shared
A personal habit is not culture. It must belong to a group. A collective pattern — that is what makes something cultural rather than merely individual.
🏛️
03
Cumulative
Culture builds across generations. Each generation inherits and adds. This is why old cultures feel deep — India, for example, carries thousands of years of accumulation.
🔄
04
Dynamic
Culture changes. No culture is frozen. Contact, migration, globalization — all of these reshape culture constantly. We need a living ability to keep reading the context, not a fixed cultural map.
🌐
05
Holistic
Culture covers everything — time, God, money, illness, death. It is not one part of life. It is all of life. To understand one part, you need to see the whole system.

Key Missiological Implication

SLIDE 22
Most Important Concept of Lecture 2

Conversion is not just a change in one belief. It is a total cultural disruption.

When someone comes to faith in Christ, far more is happening than a doctrinal shift. Cultural understanding is not background preparation for ministry — it is front-line skill.

Preview: Emic & Etic Perspectives

SLIDE 23
Insider View
Emic
How do people inside a culture see their own world? What does it mean to them? Why do they do what they do?
Outsider View
Etic
Comparing one culture with others. Analyzing patterns from the outside. Cross-cultural perspective and pattern recognition.

Emic alone → no comparison. Etic alone → no depth. Together → real understanding begins. We develop both in Weeks 2 and beyond.
